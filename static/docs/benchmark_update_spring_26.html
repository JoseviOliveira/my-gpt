Abstract

This update compares four older local deployment candidates (qwen3:4b, qwen3:8b, gemma3:4b, gemma3:12b) against four spring 2026 successors (qwen3.5:4b, qwen3.5:9b, gemma4:latest, gemma4:26b) on the same canonical 8-dataset / 80-task suite.

The report uses the final benchmark outcomes stored in the database for all eight models and the canonical 8-dataset / 80-task suite.

The final distribution is 416/640 passed (65.0%) and 224/640 failed (35.0%) across the selected eight-model comparison set.

1. Introduction

1.1 Motivation

The objective is to compare whether spring 2026 model upgrades materially improve product-fit for fast local chat, not just isolated benchmark correctness.

1.2 Scope

Scope covers eight models over eight datasets (five shortform + three chat), totaling 640 evaluations on the canonical 80-task family.

1.3 Comparison Rule

All comparisons in this page use the final benchmark results recorded for each model and dataset. The purpose is to compare release candidates as they stand in the database, not intermediate evaluation artifacts.

1.4 Practical Interpretation

This page should be read as a release-comparison report. It asks whether the newer qwen and gemma families outperform their older local deployment counterparts when speed, chat discipline, and thermal cost are considered together.

2. Methodology

2.1 Five Principal Axes

⚡

Axis 1: Responsiveness (TTFT)

Lower TTFT maps to higher normalized score.

🌊

Axis 2: Streaming Quality (tok/s)

Higher sustained token throughput maps to higher score.

💬

Axis 3: Chat Accuracy

Measured as pass rate over chat_instruction, chat_memory, and chat_safety.

🎯

Axis 4: Task Versatility

Decomposed into Math (cs+physics), Reasoning (critical+logic), and Instruction (chat_instruction).

🌡️

Axis 5: Thermal Stability

Lower mean GPU temperature maps to higher normalized thermal score.

2.2 Scoring Formula

Composite fitness follows the same guided-report weighting:

fitness = 0.50 × chat_ux + 0.30 × speed + 0.20 × shortform_quality

where speed = 0.6 × TTFT_score + 0.4 × throughput_score.

9. Conclusion

What Changed in Spring '26

The new cohort is not a uniform upgrade. On average it improves chat quality and slightly edges the baseline on average shortform quality, but those gains are overwhelmed in several models by TTFT inflation and thermal cost.

Gemma family: gemma4:latest is the most convincing spring release because it improves on baseline shortform quality without collapsing completely on responsiveness. gemma4:26b is highly accurate, but its 75.6s mean TTFT makes it difficult to justify for fast local chat UX.

Qwen family: the qwen3.5 releases clean up chat behavior versus the old qwen3 line, especially on memory and safety, but they regress too sharply on latency and thermal profile. In this benchmark family, qwen3:8b remains the better deployment compromise.

Bottom line: spring 2026 produced one meaningful new production candidate in gemma4:latest, but it did not displace gemma3:4b as the overall best local product-fit model in this guided benchmark framework.

11. Higher-Level Reading

Provider messaging around the spring 2026 upgrades emphasizes broader capability: stronger reasoning, more agentic behavior, longer context handling, richer tool use, and more ambitious model designs. Those are legitimate improvements, but they are not identical to the optimization target of this benchmark.

Our benchmark is narrower and more product-facing. It rewards fast first-token response, sustainable streaming, stable thermals, disciplined chat behavior, and enough reasoning quality to hold up in everyday local use. Under those constraints, a model can improve in the provider's intended direction and still become a worse deployment choice for this app.

That is the main lesson from this update. gemma4:latest shows that some of the new capability work can translate into a meaningful product candidate, but the broader spring cohort confirms that more capable and more complex does not automatically mean better for a local assistant with strict UX and hardware limits.
